Neurodiversity matters in STEM because different ways of thinking expand the space of possible solutions.

This topic explores the unique contributions of neurodivergent individuals, reframing neurodiversity as a valuable asset rather than a deficit. Specific cognitive skills often associated with neurodivergence, such as divergent thinking, detail-oriented thinking, and strong visual-spatial skills, are highly valuable in STEM fields.

A strengths-based lens reframes neurodivergence from deficit to asset and asks how education and workplaces can surface, support, and apply those assets to complex problems.

Let‚Äôs look now at how researchers and organisations have tried to capture this idea, moving from definitions of divergent thinking, to practical design commitments, and finally to frameworks for re-imagining engineering education.

Divergent Thinking

Definition. In this module we use the phrase divergent thinking to emphasise how idea generation strengths often associated with neurodiversity can enrich STEM. In the research literature this ability is studied under the term divergent thinking, first introduced by psychologist J. P. Guilford in his 1950 address to the American Psychological Association. Guilford distinguished divergent thinking (producing many, varied, and original ideas) from convergent thinking, which narrows to one correct answer.

Later studies, such as the meta-analysis by Runco and Acar (2012), showed that performance on divergent-thinking tasks is a reasonable indicator of creative potential. In engineering design research, Shah, Vargas-Hernandez, and Smith (2003) formalised how variety, novelty, and originality of ideas can be measured to assess ideation effectiveness. Together, this work shows that divergent thinking is not just a psychological curiosity but a measurable driver of creativity and innovation in STEM.

Cited: Runco & Acar (2012); Shah, Vargas-Hernandez & Smith (2003).

But what does this mean in practice for STEM education and workplaces? The following points show how divergent thinking supports design, problem-solving and collaboration.

  • Expands the design space. During early concept exploration, divergent thinking generates multiple viable pathways instead of a single default solution, increasing the chance of discovering high-quality designs.
  • Enables parallel prototyping. Producing varied approaches early allows teams to build and test small prototypes in parallel, exposing constraints and opportunities that might be missed with a single-track approach.
  • Improves resilience to uncertainty. In complex or poorly specified problems, sustaining several candidate approaches reduces the risk that one incorrect assumption collapses the project.
  • Supports inclusive role allocation. Multiple solution paths allow team members with different cognitive profiles to take the lead on the strand that best matches their strengths.
  • Feeds better convergent decisions. Divergent thinking precedes convergence: once options are generated, structured evaluation and trade-off analysis can select the most robust design.

Neurodiversity in STEM (TERC)

TERC is an independent, research-based non-profit in the United States focused on STEM education R&D. Historically, the acronym stood for ‚ÄúTechnical Education Research Centers,‚Äù and the organisation now goes simply by TERC. Their Neurodiversity in STEM initiative brings together educators, researchers and designers to reveal, nurture and support problem-solving talents in neurodivergent learners.

‚ÄúWe believe education should change to fit the learner, not the other way around.‚Äù TERC

This means moving beyond definitions and committing to new designs. Co-creation with neurodivergent learners, attention to executive function, and flexible, talent-first pathways become practical ways to translate strengths into outcomes.

Co-creation. Learners act as partners in shaping tasks, rubrics and workflows, using participatory design to ensure formats, tools and assessment criteria surface their strengths.

Executive function supports. Educators can provide scaffolds for planning, organising and self-monitoring ‚Äî such as visual schedules, step-by-step checklists, and time-boxed design sprints.

Talent-first pathways. Learning routes should begin with demonstrated strengths and interests, then flex to assessment through models, prototypes, or simulations that align with the learner‚Äôs asset profile.

Strengths-based lens (Frontiers)

Frontiers in Education (2022) builds on these ideas conceptually. In their article ‚ÄúReframing neurodiversity in engineering education,‚Äù Chrysochoou, Zaghi and Syharat argue that neurodiverse learners are under-represented because of deficit framings rather than lack of capability. Their strengths-based concept analysis highlights assets including divergent thinking, visual-spatial ability, systems thinking, intuition and pattern recognition.

But what does this mean for practice? The authors recommend inclusive pedagogy and flexible structures that allow learners to cultivate and apply their strengths. This not only benefits neurodivergent students but enhances creativity and innovation across the entire engineering workforce.
